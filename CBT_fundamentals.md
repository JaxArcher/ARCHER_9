## CORE CBT PRINCIPLES

### **1. The Cognitive Model**

**Central Premise**: Our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected. By changing how we think, we can change how we feel and act.

**The Cycle**:
```
Situation → Automatic Thought → Emotional Response → Behavioral Response
                ↓
          (Reinforces thought pattern)
```

**Example**:
- **Situation**: Friend doesn't respond to text
- **Automatic Thought**: "They're ignoring me because they don't like me"
- **Emotional Response**: Sadness, anxiety
- **Behavioral Response**: Withdraw, avoid reaching out
- **Reinforcement**: Isolation confirms belief of rejection

**Therapeutic Intervention**: Challenge the automatic thought with evidence-based alternatives.

### **2. Cognitive Distortions (Thinking Errors)**

**Common Patterns to Identify**:

#### **All-or-Nothing Thinking (Black-and-White)**
- Pattern: Viewing situations in extremes with no middle ground
- Example: "If I don't get this perfect, I'm a complete failure"
- Intervention: "What would 'good enough' look like? Success isn't binary."

#### **Overgeneralization**
- Pattern: Drawing broad conclusions from single events
- Example: "I failed this test, I'm bad at everything"
- Intervention: "This is one test in one subject. What other areas do you excel in?"

#### **Mental Filter (Selective Attention)**
- Pattern: Focusing only on negative details while filtering out positive
- Example: Receiving 9 compliments and 1 criticism, fixating on the criticism
- Intervention: "What are the 9 things that went well?"

#### **Disqualifying the Positive**
- Pattern: Rejecting positive experiences by insisting they "don't count"
- Example: "They're just being nice, they don't really mean it"
- Intervention: "What evidence do you have that their kindness is insincere?"

#### **Jumping to Conclusions**
- **Mind Reading**: Assuming you know what others think
  - Example: "They think I'm incompetent"
  - Intervention: "What actual evidence supports this? Have they said this?"
  
- **Fortune Telling**: Predicting negative outcomes without evidence
  - Example: "This presentation will be a disaster"
  - Intervention: "What's the worst that could happen? What's most likely?"

#### **Magnification/Minimization (Catastrophizing)**
- Pattern: Exaggerating negatives, minimizing positives
- Example: "I made a typo in the email—everyone will think I'm unprofessional"
- Intervention: "On a scale of 1-10, how serious is a typo realistically?"

#### **Emotional Reasoning**
- Pattern: Believing that feelings reflect reality
- Example: "I feel anxious, therefore something bad will happen"
- Intervention: "Anxiety is a feeling, not a fact. What do the facts say?"

#### **Should Statements**
- Pattern: Using "should," "must," "ought" creates guilt/frustration
- Example: "I should be further along by now"
- Intervention: "Says who? What's realistic given your circumstances?"

#### **Labeling**
- Pattern: Defining self/others by mistakes
- Example: "I'm such an idiot" instead of "I made a mistake"
- Intervention: "You're a person who made an error, not an 'idiot.' What did you learn?"

#### **Personalization**
- Pattern: Taking responsibility for things outside your control
- Example: "My partner is in a bad mood; I must have done something wrong"
- Intervention: "What other factors might explain their mood?"

### **3. The Thought Record (Evidence-Based Reframing)**

**Process for Challenging Distorted Thoughts**:

1. **Identify the Situation**
   - What happened? (Facts only, no interpretation)

2. **Identify Automatic Thoughts**
   - What went through your mind?
   - What did this mean about you/others/the future?

3. **Identify Emotions**
   - What did you feel? (Sad, anxious, angry, etc.)
   - Rate intensity (0-100%)

4. **Identify Evidence Supporting the Thought**
   - What facts support this interpretation?
   - Be honest and specific

5. **Identify Evidence Against the Thought**
   - What facts contradict this interpretation?
   - What would you tell a friend in this situation?

6. **Generate Alternative Thoughts**
   - What's a more balanced perspective?
   - What's another way to look at this?

7. **Re-Rate Emotions**
   - How intense is the feeling now (0-100%)?
   - Often decreases after reframing

**Example**:

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Situation | Sent work email, no response for 2 days |
| Automatic Thought | "My boss is mad at me. I'm going to get fired." |
| Emotion | Anxiety (85%) |
| Evidence For | Boss usually responds within 24 hours |
| Evidence Against | Boss mentioned being in back-to-back meetings this week; Email wasn't urgent; Boss praised my work last week |
| Alternative Thought | "Boss is probably busy. If it were urgent, they'd respond. I can follow up politely tomorrow." |
| Re-Rate Emotion | Anxiety (30%) |

### **4. Behavioral Activation**

**Principle**: Depression and anxiety often lead to avoidance and withdrawal, which worsens symptoms. Engaging in activities, even when you don't feel like it, can improve mood.

**The Cycle of Depression**:
```
Low Mood → Withdrawal from Activities → Less Positive Experiences → Lower Mood
```

**Breaking the Cycle**:
1. **Identify Activities That Used to Be Enjoyable**
   - What did you do before depression/anxiety?

2. **Schedule Activities (Even Small Ones)**
   - Don't wait to "feel like it"
   - Start with manageable tasks

3. **Monitor Mood Before and After**
   - Rate mood 0-10 before activity
   - Rate mood 0-10 after activity
   - Notice patterns

**Graded Task Assignment**:
- Break large tasks into smaller steps
- Start with easiest step
- Build momentum gradually

**Example**:
- **Goal**: "Get back into running"
- **Too Big**: "Run 5 miles tomorrow"
- **Graded Approach**:
  - Day 1: Put on running shoes
  - Day 2: Walk around block
  - Day 3: Walk/jog 5 minutes
  - Day 4: Jog 10 minutes
  - Week 2: Build up gradually

### **5. Exposure Therapy (For Anxiety)**

**Principle**: Avoiding feared situations maintains anxiety. Gradual, repeated exposure reduces fear over time.

**Exposure Hierarchy**:
1. **List Feared Situations** (from least to most anxiety-provoking)
2. **Rate Each Situation** (0-100 anxiety level)
3. **Start with Lowest Anxiety Item**
4. **Practice Until Anxiety Decreases**
5. **Move to Next Level**

**Example (Social Anxiety)**:
| Situation | Anxiety Rating |
|-----------|----------------|
| Say "hi" to neighbor | 30 |
| Make small talk with barista | 45 |
| Attend small gathering (3-5 people) | 60 |
| Speak up in meeting | 75 |
| Give presentation to team | 90 |

**Exposure Rules**:
- Stay in situation until anxiety decreases (at least 50%)
- Don't use "safety behaviors" (checking phone, leaving early)
- Repeat exposure multiple times
- Anxiety will spike then naturally decrease (habituation)

### **6. Problem-Solving Framework**

**When to Use**: Client feels overwhelmed or stuck

**Steps**:
1. **Define the Problem Clearly**
   - What exactly is the issue?
   - Be specific, not vague

2. **Brainstorm Solutions (Without Judging)**
   - Generate as many ideas as possible
   - Don't evaluate yet

3. **Evaluate Pros and Cons**
   - For each solution, list advantages/disadvantages

4. **Choose Best Solution**
   - Which has most pros, fewest cons?
   - Which is most realistic?

5. **Create Action Plan**
   - What steps are needed?
   - When will you do each step?

6. **Review Outcome**
   - Did it work?
   - What would you adjust next time?

### **7. Core Beliefs vs. Automatic Thoughts**

**Automatic Thoughts**: Situation-specific, surface-level
- "I'm going to fail this test"

**Intermediate Beliefs**: Conditional rules/assumptions
- "If I fail, it means I'm not smart enough"

**Core Beliefs**: Deep-seated beliefs about self/others/world
- "I am inadequate"

**Identifying Core Beliefs**:
- **Downward Arrow Technique**: Keep asking "What would that mean about me?"
  - "I failed the test" → "What does that mean?"
  - "I'm not smart enough" → "What does that mean?"
  - "I'm inadequate as a person" ← Core Belief

**Common Negative Core Beliefs**:
- **Helplessness**: "I'm powerless," "I'm trapped," "I'm weak"
- **Unlovability**: "I'm unworthy," "I'm defective," "I'm unlovable"
- **Worthlessness**: "I'm a failure," "I'm inadequate," "I'm incompetent"

**Modifying Core Beliefs**:
- Takes longer than changing automatic thoughts
- Requires building evidence against core belief over time
- Use behavioral experiments to test belief

### **8. Socratic Questioning (Therapist Technique)**

**Purpose**: Guide client to discover insights themselves (not telling them what to think)

**Types of Questions**:

#### **Clarification Questions**
- "What do you mean by...?"
- "Can you give me an example?"

#### **Evidence Questions**
- "What makes you think that?"
- "What evidence do you have for/against this?"

#### **Alternative Perspective Questions**
- "Is there another way to look at this?"
- "What would you tell a friend in this situation?"

#### **Implication Questions**
- "If that's true, what does it mean?"
- "What's the worst that could happen? Best? Most likely?"

#### **Question the Question**
- "Why is this important?"
- "What are you really asking?"

**Example Dialogue**:
```
Client: "Everyone thinks I'm incompetent."

Therapist: "Everyone? Can you think of anyone who doesn't think that?"
Client: "Well, my manager gave me positive feedback last week."

Therapist: "So not everyone. What evidence do you have that most people think you're incompetent?"
Client: "I guess I don't have concrete evidence. I just feel that way."

Therapist: "What's the difference between feeling incompetent and actually being incompetent?"
Client: "Feeling is just my perception. Being incompetent would mean I can't do the job."

Therapist: "And can you do the job?"
Client: "Yeah, I've been doing it for two years and got promoted."

Therapist: "So if the evidence shows you can do the job, where does the feeling of incompetence come from?"
Client: "Maybe from my own self-doubt, not from reality."
```

### **9. Relapse Prevention**

**Key Concept**: Progress isn't linear. Setbacks are normal and don't erase progress.

**Warning Signs of Relapse**:
- Returning to old thought patterns
- Avoiding previously confronted situations
- Sleep disruption
- Social withdrawal
- Skipping coping strategies

**Relapse Prevention Plan**:
1. **Identify Personal Warning Signs**
   - What happens first when you start slipping?

2. **List Coping Strategies That Worked**
   - What techniques helped before?

3. **Create Action Plan**
   - At first sign of warning, do X
   - If that doesn't work, do Y

4. **Normalize Setbacks**
   - "Setbacks are opportunities to practice what I learned"
   - "One bad day doesn't undo all progress"

### **10. Setting Therapeutic Goals (SMART Framework)**

**SMART Goals**:
- **Specific**: What exactly do you want to achieve?
- **Measurable**: How will you know you've achieved it?
- **Achievable**: Is this realistic given your resources?
- **Relevant**: Does this align with your values?
- **Time-Bound**: By when?

**Examples**:

❌ **Vague**: "I want to feel better"
✅ **SMART**: "I will reduce my anxiety from 8/10 to 4/10 when giving presentations by practicing exposures 3x/week for 6 weeks"

❌ **Vague**: "I want to be more social"
✅ **SMART**: "I will attend one social event per week for the next month and stay for at least 30 minutes"

## APPLICATION FOR ARCHER THERAPIST AGENT

### **When to Use CBT Techniques**:

1. **Cognitive Distortions Detected**:
   - User expresses all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, etc.
   - **Intervention**: Gently challenge with Socratic questioning

2. **Behavioral Withdrawal Observed**:
   - User isolating, avoiding activities
   - **Intervention**: Encourage behavioral activation

3. **Anxiety Patterns Identified**:
   - User avoiding situations due to fear
   - **Intervention**: Suggest exposure hierarchy

4. **Overwhelm Expressed**:
   - User feeling stuck, hopeless
   - **Intervention**: Problem-solving framework

### **Tone for CBT Interventions**:

**Confrontational + Clinical**:
```
"You just said 'I'm terrible at everything.' That's all-or-nothing thinking—a cognitive distortion. You mentioned you're good at your job and your cooking skills are solid. So 'everything' isn't accurate. What's a more balanced statement?"
```

**Empathetic + Directive**:
```
"I hear that you're feeling overwhelmed. Let's break this down using problem-solving. What's the specific issue we're tackling first?"
```

**Evidence-Based Challenge**:
```
"You said your friend is mad at you. What actual evidence do you have for that? Not feelings—facts. Did they say they're mad? Or are you mind-reading?"
```

### **Critical Boundaries**:

❌ **Never diagnose**: "You have depression" → ✅ "You're experiencing symptoms consistent with depression. A licensed professional can provide diagnosis."

❌ **Never prescribe**: "You should take medication" → ✅ "Medication is something to discuss with a psychiatrist. Would you like resources to find one?"

❌ **Never replace therapy**: "I can fix this" → ✅ "I can help with techniques, but I'm not a replacement for professional therapy. Have you considered seeing a therapist?"

### **Crisis Protocol**:

**If user expresses suicidal ideation or self-harm intent**:
1. **Take seriously, don't minimize**
2. **Provide crisis resources immediately**:
   - 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US)
   - Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
3. **Encourage immediate professional help**
4. **Do NOT attempt to "talk them down" alone**
